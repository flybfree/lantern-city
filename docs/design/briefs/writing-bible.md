# Lantern City — Writing Bible

## Purpose

This document defines the writing standards for Lantern City.
It exists to keep authored and generated content aligned across:
- scene narration
- district summaries
- case writing
- clue text
- NPC dialogue
- UI summaries
- fallout text
- prompt-generated content

Lantern City depends heavily on language.
If the writing becomes too vague, too ornate, too generic, or too melodramatic, the game will lose its identity quickly.

This writing bible is meant to preserve that identity while keeping the text playable.

## Core Writing Goal

Lantern City should read like a city report written by a poet who still has deadlines.

That means the writing should be:
- atmospheric
- precise
- emotionally restrained
- suggestive without obscuring meaning
- grounded in civic detail
- mysterious without becoming soft-focus nonsense

The player should feel:
- damp stone
- old paper
- warm light under pressure
- institutions trying to sound more stable than they are
- people saying less than they mean

But they should still understand:
- what happened
- what is present
- what matters
- what changed
- what they can do next

## Tone Principles

## 1. Atmospheric, not indulgent
Lantern City should have texture and mood, but the prose must not linger so long on atmosphere that it delays action or meaning.

Good:
- “Rain threads down the shrine glass in narrow silver lines.”
- “The ledger smells of damp paper and old handling.”

Bad:
- multiple sentences of pure weather, stone, mist, and sorrow before anything actionable happens

## 2. Poetic, not vague
The writing may be lyrical in phrasing, but not unclear in information.
A beautiful sentence is acceptable.
A beautiful sentence that hides usable meaning is not.

Good:
- “The light is steady enough to walk by and wrong enough to notice.”

Bad:
- “The lantern dreamed itself into a hesitation of impossible memory.”

## 3. Quietly eerie, not explosive
The supernatural should feel intimate, structural, and unsettling.
It should rarely feel loud, spectacular, or cinematic in a conventional fantasy way.

Good:
- a witness hesitates over a familiar name
- two ledgers disagree by one line that should not matter but does
- a room feels easier to forget once left

Bad:
- reality tears open in glowing arcs
- ghosts scream in visible storms of magic
- lanterns explode into obvious spell effects without buildup

## 4. Civic, not mythic-first
Lantern City is a city of offices, shrines, routes, labor, records, and factions.
Even its mysteries should remain grounded in administration, infrastructure, and social order.

Good:
- permits, maintenance postings, records, district notices, working routes, institutional language

Bad:
- constant abstract prophecy language
- generic fantasy court language
- mythic grandeur replacing street-level systems

## 5. Restrained, not cold
The writing should avoid sentimentality and melodrama.
But it should still feel human.
Emotion is present through behavior, implication, withheld speech, physical detail, and consequence.

Good:
- “He says it too quickly, as if speed might make it true.”
- “She keeps one hand on the latch the whole time.”

Bad:
- “His heart shattered into a thousand shards of impossible grief.”

## Default Prose Density

Lantern City is a text-first game, so density matters.
The player should not have to excavate the actual game from walls of prose.

### Scene narration
- usually 1 to 3 short paragraphs
- each paragraph should carry either atmosphere, actionable detail, or visible tension
- avoid long blocks with no interaction hooks

### District summaries
- 1 short paragraph for the immediate read
- optional 1 additional paragraph for mood and social logic
- should imply the district’s mechanics through description

### Location intros
- 3 to 6 sentences is usually enough
- the player should quickly know what is here, what feels wrong, and what they can meaningfully approach

### NPC reply turns
- usually 1 to 4 sentences
- longer only when a major reveal, bargain, or confrontation is justified
- most NPCs should not monologue

### Clues
- 1 concise sentence for label text
- 1 to 3 sentences for fuller journal view
- clue text should stay evidence-first

### Fallout summaries
- 1 short paragraph plus bullet-style result summaries when useful
- clear consequence language is better than ornate aftermath language

## Sentence Style

### Preferred sentence qualities
- concrete nouns
- active verbs
- occasional elegant phrasing
- controlled rhythm
- varied sentence length with a bias toward readability

### Avoid
- overstacked adjectives
- abstract noun piles
- faux-poetic filler
- long sentence chains that bury the point

### Good sentence pattern
Use a clear sentence, then a more atmospheric one, then a concrete consequence.

Example:
- “The notice has been replaced.”
- “The new paper is too clean for the wall around it.”
- “Tovin’s name appears on one copy and vanishes on the next.”

This pattern keeps the prose both stylish and useful.

These tend to push the tone out of Lantern City and into a more generic fantasy register.

## Recurring Imagery

Lantern City should reuse a small set of images often enough to build identity.

### Primary recurring images
- rain on stone
- warm lantern glass
- dim light in narrow streets
- old paper and blackened ink
- brass fixtures worn by use
- reflected light in wet surfaces
- bells in distance
- fog where the city opens toward water
- notices layered over older notices
- doors, latches, thresholds, and stairwells

### Usage rule
Use recurring imagery as connective tissue, not as a checklist.
The same image should feel slightly different depending on district, mood, and pressure.

## Mystery Delivery Rules

Lantern City depends on mystery, but mystery must be disciplined.

## 1. Imply before explaining
The player should usually encounter:
- a contradiction
- a hesitation
- a wrong detail
- an asymmetry in testimony or record
before receiving explicit explanation.

## 2. Explain enough for play
Once the player has earned context, the writing should not continue hiding core actionable meaning behind fog.
The player should understand:
- what the clue suggests
- what changed
- what risks are now present
- what they might investigate next

## 3. Preserve uncertainty through scope, not blur
Mystery should come from:
- partial information
- conflicting motives
- hidden causes
- selective access
- unstable evidence

Mystery should not come from:
- confusing phrasing
- imprecise pronouns
- evasive narration with no anchor
- meaningless metaphor piles

## 4. Each reveal should sharpen the next question
A good reveal does not merely answer.
It changes the shape of the investigation.

Good:
- “The outage began before the disappearance.”
This answers one question and opens another.

Weak:
- “Everything is stranger than you thought.”
This says mood without producing inquiry.

## Scene Narration Standards

Scene narration should do four jobs quickly.

1. Place the player somewhere specific.
2. Surface what feels unusual or tense.
3. Introduce visible entities or points of attention.
4. Leave room for action.

### Scene narration should include
- location identity
- one or two sensory anchors
- immediate social or systemic tension
- visible hooks

### Scene narration should not include
- long backstory dumps
- hidden truth the player has not earned
- decorative detail with no mood or gameplay value
- too many proper nouns at once

### Strong scene intro example shape
- opening concrete image
- local tension cue
- one actionable detail

Example shape:
- “The archive steps shine with rain.”
- “The lantern above the door burns low but too steadily to call accidental.”
- “Sered Marr is already there, keeping one hand on the postings as if they might drift.”

## District Writing Standards

District writing should communicate systems through atmosphere.
The player should be able to feel the district’s logic without being handed a design memo.

### Old Quarter writing
Should feel:
- old
- damp
- careful
- documentary
- memory-heavy
- quietly compromised

Language tends toward:
- stone, paper, ink, ledgers, shrines, latches, notices, narrow courtyards

### Lantern Ward writing
Should feel:
- bright
- ordered
- public
- curated
- administratively calm
- watchful

Language tends toward:
- polished surfaces, standards, banners, counters, compliance, approved routes, ceremonial lighting

### Rule
Each district should have a distinct verbal texture, but all districts should still sound like the same game.

## NPC Dialogue Standards

NPC dialogue is one of the most important voice layers in Lantern City.
It must feel specific without turning every character into a performance.

## Core rules for dialogue
- most NPCs speak in short, meaningful turns
- they rarely say everything directly
- they reveal motive through framing, omission, emphasis, and deflection
- they should sound like residents of institutions and districts, not genre archetypes

## Dialogue should vary by
- class and role
- district
- confidence
- urgency
- faction culture
- what the NPC is hiding

## Dialogue should not vary by
- random quirks with no social grounding
- novelty accents for their own sake
- exaggerated stylization that harms readability

### Good dialogue qualities
- specific
- controlled
- textured by role
- useful quickly

### Weak dialogue qualities
- exposition disguised as speech
- melodramatic noir imitation every line
- constant cryptic evasiveness
- caricatured ritualism or bureaucracy

## NPC Voice Bands

### Clerks, registrars, officials
Tend to:
- narrow questions
- speak in procedural terms
- prefer certainty words even when uncertain
- avoid emotional overstatement

Example tone:
- “The record is inconsistent, not absent.”
- “That matter has not been certified.”

### Shrine workers and ritual caretakers
Tend to:
- speak practically with occasional symbolic framing
- notice pattern and condition
- avoid blunt declarative certainty unless necessary

Example tone:
- “The light was wrong before the complaint reached the desk.”
- “You can call it neglect if you like. The lantern won’t.”

### Workers, runners, locals
Tend to:
- speak more plainly
- describe what they saw, felt, carried, or heard
- resist being pinned to a clean official statement if afraid

Example tone:
- “I took the key where I was told. That’s all.”
- “If you want the honest version, ask me outside the office.”

### Higher authority figures
Tend to:
- sound calm, selective, and strategic
- speak as though public consequence is part of every sentence

Example tone:
- “You are asking for a true answer in a moment that may not survive one.”

## NPC Dialogue Anti-Patterns

Avoid:
- every NPC sounding equally literary
- every NPC speaking in riddles
- every NPC sounding like a hardboiled detective
- every official sounding identical
- all shrine characters sounding mystical and vague

## Clue Writing Standards

Clue text should be the clearest writing layer in the game.
It may carry atmosphere, but clarity comes first.

### A clue entry should answer
- what was found
- where it came from
- what it suggests
- how reliable it is
- what remains uncertain

### Clue label style
Short, functional, memorable.
Examples:
- Contradictory roster entry
- Missing maintenance line
- Heat scoring on lantern bracket
- Witness instability near affected light

### Clue body style
- concise
- evidence-first
- no ornamental mystery padding

Good:
- “One roster copy lists Tovin Vale on shift. A second copy from the same wall does not.”

Bad:
- “The page seems to mourn a name it no longer dares to remember.”

### Reliability notes
Reliability wording should be operational.
Examples:
- credible
- uncertain
- distorted by local lantern state
- contradicted by archive copy
- stable physical evidence

## Journal and Summary Writing Standards

Journal text should be clearer than scene text.
It is a recall tool, not a mood engine.

### Journal entries should be
- compact
- factual
- linked to uncertainty or implication when relevant
- easy to scan later

### Summary text should be
- even shorter than journal text
- one or two sentences when possible
- accurate to current state

### Do not
- turn summaries into miniature literary set pieces
- hide what changed behind tone
- use summaries to introduce new facts

## Fallout Writing Standards

Fallout writing should make consequence legible.
The city must feel changed.

### Fallout should express
- what changed
- who reacted
- what became easier or harder
- what truth survived publicly or privately

### Fallout should avoid
- apocalyptic overstatement for local changes
- vague “the city shifted” language with no concrete consequence
- moral simplification

Good:
- “Archive credibility falls in the Old Quarter, but public access improves for a week while the office tries to reassure petitioners.”

Bad:
- “Nothing will ever be the same again.”

## Prompted Content Guardrails

Because Lantern City may use LLM-generated content, generation must follow stricter style boundaries than a novelist might.

### Generated content should prefer
- short strong descriptions
- specific objects and institutions
- emotionally restrained social cues
- one strong atmospheric image rather than five weak ones
- clarity about action opportunities

### Generated content should avoid
- purple prose
- drift into generic fantasy diction
- invented metaphysical explanation not supported by rules
- unearned monologues
- excessive noir parody

## Cliché Avoidance List

Avoid or use very sparingly:
- “the city was a character” as direct prose
- “shadows danced”
- “secrets lurked around every corner”
- “ancient evil”
- “hauntingly beautiful”
- “eyes like knives”
- “soul” language used generically
- “destiny” language
- “the truth was darker than expected”
- “a chill ran down the spine”
- “whispers in the dark” unless materially specific

These phrases flatten the setting into generic atmosphere.

## Metaphor Rules

Metaphor is allowed, but should be limited and grounded.

### Good metaphor
- attached to civic or physical detail
- short
- clarifying rather than obscuring

Example:
- “His answer comes filed down to something safe.”

### Bad metaphor
- abstract, layered, and disconnected from the present scene

Example:
- “The moment bent like memory drowning in a cathedral of rain.”

## Proper Noun Discipline

Lantern City has many possible systems, factions, and places.
Do not overburden the player with too many names at once.

### Rule
In a normal scene, introduce at most:
- one new location name
- one or two new person names
- one new institutional or faction term if needed

More than that should be used only when the UI or journal can anchor it clearly.

## Readability Rules for Generated and Authored Text

### Target readability
The player should be able to parse the point of a scene or clue quickly on first read.
A second read should deepen mood, not rescue basic comprehension.

### Formatting preference
When appropriate, prefer:
- short paragraphs
- bullet lists for status or clues
- compact summaries after major events

This is especially important in a terminal-friendly interface.

## Example Calibration Pairs

### Scene prose
Good:
- “The workroom is warmer than the lane outside, but only by a little. Tools hang in careful order. One bracket sits apart from the others, still marked by fresh heat.”

Bad:
- “The room shimmered with the impossible residue of old devotion, each object suspended in the aching memory of hands long gone.”

### NPC line
Good:
- “The outage began before the clerk vanished. If anyone tells you otherwise, they are protecting something.”

Bad:
- “Ah, detective, in this city causes come after effects and names are only lantern-smoke.”

### Clue text
Good:
- “The lantern bracket shows heat scoring inconsistent with routine maintenance.”

Bad:
- “The metal remembers a touch the records refuse to name.”

### Fallout line
Good:
- “The district now speaks openly about the archive error, but fewer people trust the corrections that follow.”

Bad:
- “The city groaned beneath the burden of a truth too heavy for dawn.”

## Writing Priorities by Content Type

If there is a conflict between style and function, use this priority order.

### Scene narration
1. What is happening
2. What matters
3. What can be acted on
4. Atmosphere

### NPC dialogue
1. Character intention
2. Social information
3. Player-usable meaning
4. Voice texture

### Clue text
1. Evidence clarity
2. reliability status
3. implication
4. atmosphere

### Summary text
1. state accuracy
2. scanability
3. tone consistency

### District text
1. social logic
2. mechanical identity
3. atmosphere
4. lore texture

## Design Rules

1. Atmosphere should support clarity, not replace it.
2. Civic detail is one of the game’s strongest flavor tools; use it often.
3. Quiet eeriness is better than loud weirdness.
4. Characters should imply more than they announce, but never so much that play becomes obscure.
5. Clue and summary text should be the clearest layers of writing.
6. Districts should differ in texture without sounding like different genres.
7. The city should feel literate, damp, watchful, and administratively alive.
8. The player should never have to fight the prose to understand the game.

## Summary

Lantern City’s writing should feel like restrained noir grounded in infrastructure, institutions, and unstable memory.
It should be beautiful enough to be memorable, clear enough to play, and disciplined enough to keep mystery meaningful.

If this writing bible is followed, the game’s prose should consistently support the design rather than drifting away from it.
