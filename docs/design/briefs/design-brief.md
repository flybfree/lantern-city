# Lantern City — Design Brief

## 1. High Concept

Lantern City is a text-first RPG set in a rain-soaked port metropolis where lanterns are tied to memory, civic order, and reality itself. The game centers on investigation, negotiation, and the slow unraveling of a city where people, records, and even places can go missing.

The player takes the role of a wandering investigator/fixer who moves between districts, learns how the city works, and intervenes when something strange threatens the balance of power.

## 2. Design Intent

The project is meant to teach agentic AI through play.

The key idea is that the world is made of visible agents:
- factions with goals
- NPCs with memories and objectives
- districts with local rules
- systems that change state over time
- information that can be hidden, distorted, or lost

The game should make agency legible. The player should be able to see that actions have consequences, that NPCs pursue their own interests, and that the city itself behaves like a living system.

## 3. Tone and Mood

The tone should be:
- noir
- wet, dim, and atmospheric
- emotionally restrained
- poetic without becoming vague
- eerie in a quiet way
- grounded in civic detail

Recurring imagery:
- rain on stone
- warm lantern glass
- rusted iron
- fog
- salt air
- old ink
- reflected light
- bells carrying through streets

The supernatural should feel intimate and unsettling rather than explosive or flashy.

## 4. Player Fantasy

The player is not a chosen one or a city ruler. They are a fixer-investigator who succeeds through:
- reading people
- gathering clues
- making deals
- navigating institutions
- deciding what to reveal and what to hide
- using the city’s own systems against themselves

The player should feel capable and influential, but never omnipotent.

## 5. Core Game Loop

The game is built around a repeating cycle:

1. Receive a problem
2. Travel to a district or faction stronghold
3. Gather clues through conversation, observation, and actions
4. Form a theory about what is really happening
5. Intervene through persuasion, stealth, trade, ritual, or leverage
6. See the city change in response
7. Carry the fallout into the next scene

Direct force should be useful only in limited situations. The fun should come from understanding who wants what, what is hidden, and what changes when lanterns are lit, dimmed, moved, or broken.

## 6. District Structure

Lantern City is best understood as a set of neighborhoods with distinct social logic.

### Docks
- Cargo, sailors, unions, black-market shipping
- Wet alleys, customs offices, taverns, rope bridges
- Best for smuggling, labor disputes, and disappearances

### Old Quarter
- Ancient stone, memory-heavy sites, old families
- Archives, shrines, broken courtyards, fading murals
- Best for history, secrets, and haunted obligations

### Lantern Ward
- Ceremonial and political center of lantern culture
- Bright roads, regulated light, public rituals
- Best for official authority, public reputation, and social control

### Market Spires
- Trade towers, brokers, lenders, exotic imports
- Vertical wealth, contracts, fast gossip
- Best for deals, leverage, and information markets

### Salt Barrens
- Fringe district, tidal flats, abandoned infrastructure
- Few lights, lots of superstition
- Best for hidden meetings, exiles, and things the city pretends not to see

### Underways
- Tunnels, drainage routes, maintenance passages
- Old systems beneath the city
- Best for secret travel, hidden communities, and forgotten mechanisms

Each district should have:
- a governing power
- a local economy
- a social norm
- a lantern condition
- a hidden problem

## 7. Factions

Each faction should have a visible public purpose and a private agenda.

### Council of Lights
- Public role: maintain lantern order and civic stability
- Private goal: control memory, narrative, and legitimacy

### Merchant Houses
- Public role: keep trade flowing
- Private goal: monopolize routes, debts, and scarcity

### Dock Unions
- Public role: protect labor
- Private goal: defend territory and informal power

### Memory Keepers
- Public role: preserve records, names, and civic continuity
- Private goal: determine what the city is allowed to remember

### Shrine Circles
- Public role: spiritual guidance and ritual maintenance
- Private goal: shape belief through controlled mystery

### Street Networks
- Public role: none, officially
- Private goal: survive by information, favors, and invisible logistics

Design rule:
Every faction should sometimes be helpful, sometimes dangerous, and never fully transparent.

## 8. The Lantern Phenomenon

Lanterns are not just lights. They are infrastructure, ritual objects, and memory anchors.

They do three things:
- illuminate physical space
- stabilize social order
- bind memory to place

When lanterns function normally:
- people remember events more clearly
- districts feel coherent
- records, names, and routes remain stable

When lanterns fail, are tampered with, or are corrupted:
- witnesses disagree
- maps stop matching reality
- names vanish from documents
- people feel like they have lost time
- locations become harder to trust

Lantern behavior can be local rather than universal. One street may be stable while the next block becomes uncertain.

## 9. Missingness

Missingness is the central mystery of the setting.

It does not only mean someone disappeared. It can also mean:
- a person who is physically present but legally absent
- a record that exists but no one remembers making
- a family line that no longer appears in civic history
- a building that everyone agrees was always there, except the evidence says otherwise
- an event that happened, but now has no consistent account

Rules of thumb:
- Missingness is subtle before it becomes obvious
- It affects memory, institutions, and identity before it becomes supernatural spectacle
- It should create uncertainty without making everything meaningless

## 10. NPC Agent Design

Important NPCs should feel like agents with goals, memory, and constraints.

Each notable NPC should have:
- public identity
- hidden objective
- current state
- relationships
- memory of prior encounters
- a reason to act now

Example:
A dock foreman may publicly want cargo moved. Privately, they may be covering for a vanished worker, hiding a shipment, or negotiating with a rival union.

NPCs should not act like generic dialogue trees. They should:
- remember prior commitments
- update suspicion and trust
- react to evidence
- pursue goals even offscreen

## 11. State and Progression

The game needs persistent state, even if the presentation remains text-first.

Track:
- player reputation by district and faction
- unresolved mysteries
- lantern stability in each area
- key NPC states
- evidence discovered
- promises made and broken
- citywide tension

Progression should be about access, not just power:
- new districts
- new contacts
- deeper secrets
- greater influence over which version of the truth survives

## 12. Failure States

Failure should be narrative, not just a dead end.

Possible failures:
- a suspect escapes and the truth is buried
- a district becomes more unstable
- a faction turns hostile
- the player is discredited
- an NPC is erased by Missingness
- the player learns the truth too late to prevent harm

Good failure still changes the story.
It should create a new situation rather than simply stop play.

## 13. Writing Style

The writing should feel:
- atmospheric but readable
- emotionally restrained
- poetic without being opaque
- grounded in civic detail
- eerie in a quiet way

Avoid over-explaining the supernatural. Let the city feel larger than the player’s current understanding.

## 14. Design Principles

1. Truth is partial
   No one has the full story.

2. Light reveals and distorts
   Lanterns help, but they also shape what can be known.

3. Institutions remember badly
   The city’s systems are part of the mystery.

4. Every answer creates another question
   Solving one case should expose deeper structure.

5. NPCs are agents, not props
   They pursue goals and adapt.

6. The city is the real protagonist
   The player moves through a living system with history and inertia.

## 15. Example Opening Premise

A district lantern has gone dim in the Old Quarter.

At first it seems like a maintenance issue. Then a clerk reports that an entire family has no record. A shrine keeper insists the family is being edited out. Then a merchant recognizes the missing name as one attached to a debt.

The player is hired to determine whether this is:
- a bureaucratic error
- a factional cover-up
- a ritual failure
- or the first sign of a wider Missingness event

## 16. Design Goal

The game should teach agentic AI by making agency visible:
- the city has systems
- NPCs have goals
- information changes state
- decisions have persistence
- hidden objectives collide

The player should come away feeling like they were navigating a city of agents, not a static story.
