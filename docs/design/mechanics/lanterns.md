# Lantern City — Lanterns

## The Role of Lanterns

Lanterns are the central civic technology and symbolic system of Lantern City.
They are not just sources of light. They are instruments that influence:
- memory
- legitimacy
- navigation
- social order
- ritual practice
- the stability of reality in specific places

In the setting, light is political.
Who controls the lanterns controls what can be seen, remembered, and trusted.

## What Lanterns Do

### 1. Illuminate physical space
This is the obvious function. Lanterns make streets navigable, docks usable, and public spaces safe enough for commerce and movement.

### 2. Stabilize memory
Well-lit districts tend to produce more consistent witness accounts, cleaner records, and stronger continuity of identity.
When lanterns fail or are tampered with, people may remember events differently or forget them entirely.

### 3. Anchor place
Lanterns help locations remain legible.
They make routes, boundaries, and landmarks feel coherent.
Without them, streets can seem subtly wrong, maps stop matching, and familiar places become difficult to trust.

### 4. Support civic authority
The city’s institutions use lantern systems to signal order, permission, and legitimacy.
A lit district is not just brighter; it is socially approved, administratively recognized, and ritually maintained.

### 5. Shape ritual reality
Certain lanterns may be linked to shrine practice, memory rites, or legal ceremonies.
Lighting, extinguishing, relighting, or relocating a lantern can have meaning beyond the physical act.

## Lanterns as Power

Because lanterns affect what people remember and how reality feels, they are a form of power.
That power can be used to:
- confirm an official version of events
- suppress inconvenient truths
- protect a district from Missingness
- mark territory
- control access
- manipulate public confidence

This means lantern maintenance is never neutral.
Even routine upkeep has political implications.

## Lantern Conditions

Each district can have a lantern condition state.

### Bright
- stable memory
- easy navigation
- high civic visibility
- stronger institutional presence

### Dim
- uncertainty rises
- people hesitate
- rumors spread faster
- the district feels neglected or under strain

### Flickering
- reality feels unstable
- witnesses conflict
- routes feel unreliable
- Missingness pressure increases

### Extinguished
- the district becomes socially and metaphysically vulnerable
- hidden activity increases
- records degrade
- memory and identity become less reliable

### Altered
- the lantern is not broken, but changed intentionally
- the district may be governed by a different logic
- memory, access, and testimony may be selectively shaped

## Lantern Control

Different factions may control different parts of the lantern system:
- the Council of Lights regulates official lantern law
- shrine groups understand ritual maintenance
- merchants exploit lighted routes and safe passage
- dock unions depend on practical lighting for labor
- street networks know where light is absent and why

This creates a tension between public infrastructure and hidden ownership.

## Mechanical Effects

Lanterns can affect gameplay by changing:
- clue reliability
- witness consistency
- district access
- movement safety
- NPC mood and caution
- Missingness pressure
- faction confidence

A lit district should feel easier to understand.
A broken district should feel more ambiguous and dangerous.

## Investigative Use

Players may learn to use lanterns tactically.
Examples:
- restore a lantern to stabilize a memory clue
- extinguish a lantern to hide a meeting
- carry a portable light to trigger a reaction
- inspect soot, glass, or flame color for evidence of tampering
- compare how different NPCs behave under different lighting conditions

## Narrative Symbolism

Lanterns also carry theme.
They represent the tension between:
- truth and control
- clarity and distortion
- safety and surveillance
- remembrance and editing
- public order and private secrecy

The city wants light, but not necessarily truth.

## Awareness: What Characters Know

The player and NPCs should not automatically understand the full mechanics of lantern influence.
Instead, awareness should be layered.

### Common awareness
Most people in Lantern City know:
- lanterns matter
- some districts feel safer or stranger depending on their state
- certain groups control or maintain them
- dim or broken lanterns often coincide with bad events

### Skilled or informed awareness
Investigators, shrine workers, Memory Keepers, and Council agents may know more:
- which lanterns are controlled by which faction
- that lantern placement and brightness affect testimony, memory, and access
- that some lantern effects are accidental while others are deliberate
- that certain changes in light are signs of deeper manipulation

### Expert awareness
A small number of specialists may understand:
- how specific lantern systems are wired, ritually bound, or politically owned
- the difference between physical damage, ritual alteration, and memory shaping
- how far a lantern’s influence extends geographically or socially
- when a district’s problems are caused by lantern interference versus ordinary civic decay

## Control and Reach

The game should distinguish between:
- who owns or regulates a lantern
- who maintains it
- who benefits from it
- how far its influence actually reaches

These are not always the same.

Example:
- The Council of Lights may officially control a lantern grid.
- A shrine circle may secretly perform the rituals that keep it stable.
- A merchant house may benefit most from its bright trade corridor.
- The actual influence may only extend one street over, or across an entire district.

## Design Implication

The player should usually know that lanterns have influence, but not always the full scope of that influence.
That uncertainty is important.

Good play comes from discovering:
- who controls a lantern
- what they think it does
- what it actually does
- where its influence stops
- what happens when it is altered

## Lantern Knowledge Ladder

Different characters understand lanterns at different levels.
This lets the setting preserve mystery while still allowing real investigation.

### 1. Common citizens
- lanterns are part of daily life
- bright streets feel safer and more official
- dim streets feel neglected or unlucky
- people know lantern changes can mean trouble, but not why

### 2. Local workers and residents
- know which lanterns are unreliable
- know which streets go strange after dark
- may recognize patterns in outages, repairs, or strange rituals
- understand practical effects before abstract ones

### 3. District officials and competent insiders
- know which group is supposed to manage a lantern
- can identify ordinary failure versus suspicious tampering
- understand that lantern conditions affect testimony, route safety, and public confidence
- may suspect political manipulation, but not have proof

### 4. Investigators and faction agents
- know lanterns can shape memory and narrative
- understand that control, maintenance, and benefit are separate things
- can track influence across districts and institutions
- can connect lantern state to Missingness, cover-ups, or faction strategy

### 5. Ritual specialists and experts
- understand the mechanics of lantern binding, alteration, and decay
- can tell whether a lantern is broken, redirected, or ritually edited
- may know the limits of influence and the signs of hidden effects
- can attempt repairs, reversals, or deliberate manipulation

### 6. The few who know too much
- understand the deepest system behind lantern influence
- may know who built the system, why it exists, and what it is really for
- may have incentives to hide the truth from everyone else

## How the Player Gains Understanding

The player should learn about lanterns gradually through play.
Understanding should come from discovery, not exposition dump.

### Methods of learning

#### Observation
- notice how people behave differently in bright versus dim districts
- compare witness accounts across lighting conditions
- see which places feel coherent or unstable

#### Comparison
- visit multiple districts
- compare lantern conditions, social behavior, and record quality
- notice patterns in who is protected and who is exposed

#### Conversation
- ask workers, officials, shrines, and residents what they believe
- compare conflicting explanations
- identify who is minimizing, hiding, or exaggerating the truth

#### Evidence
- inspect lantern hardware, soot, glass, fuel, placement, and repair records
- find signs of tampering or ritual alteration
- trace who last handled the system

#### Consequence
- take actions that change lantern state and observe what follows
- dim, restore, relocate, or sabotage a lantern and track the results
- learn through cause and effect

#### Access
- gain trust with factions or specialists
- unlock better explanations from more informed characters
- receive documents, diagrams, or ritual knowledge

## Player Understanding Curve

The player’s knowledge should evolve in stages:

### Early game
- lanterns seem important but mysterious
- the player learns that light affects safety and social order
- clues suggest lanterns may influence memory

### Mid game
- the player realizes lanterns can be controlled politically
- they begin identifying who benefits from specific lighting conditions
- they can tie lantern behavior to missing records, false testimony, or strange district behavior

### Late game
- the player understands lanterns as a major system of power
- they can predict where influence reaches and where it breaks down
- they can use lantern knowledge to expose, defend, or manipulate the city

## Design Implication

The player should usually know that lanterns have influence, but not always the full scope of that influence.
That uncertainty is important.

Good play comes from discovering:
- who controls a lantern
- what they think it does
- what it actually does
- where its influence stops
- what happens when it is altered

## Design Rule

Whenever a lantern changes state, something in the world should also change state.
If the light changes, then memory, access, trust, or reality should shift with it.

That is what makes lanterns central rather than decorative.
