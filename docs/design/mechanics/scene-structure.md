# Lantern City — Scene Structure

## What a Scene Is

A scene is a single playable unit of interaction inside a case.
It is the smallest meaningful block of story progression.

A scene usually focuses on one of the following:
- talking to an NPC
- investigating a location
- following a clue
- making a decision
- responding to a city change
- resolving a small conflict

## Scene Purpose

A scene exists to change state.
By the end of a scene, at least one thing should be different:
- the player knows something new
- an NPC relationship changed
- access opened or closed
- a clue was gained or clarified
- a district state shifted
- a faction reacted
- the case advanced or stalled

## Scene Size

Scenes should be relatively short and focused.
A good scene usually involves:
- one setting
- one primary objective
- a small number of relevant NPCs
- one major decision or discovery

## Scene Types

### 1. Conversation Scene
A focused dialogue with one or more NPCs.
Often used to gain clues, leverage, access, or relationship changes.

### 2. Investigation Scene
The player examines a place, object, record, or event.
Often used to reveal clues or lantern-related anomalies.

### 3. Transition Scene
The player moves between locations or districts.
May include travel complications, rumors, or small discoveries.

### 4. Confrontation Scene
The player faces conflict, pressure, or opposition.
This does not have to mean combat; it can be social, political, or investigative.

### 5. Revelation Scene
A scene where the player learns something central to the case.
This usually changes the meaning of previous scenes.

### 6. Fallout Scene
A scene that shows the result of earlier choices.
Useful for making the city feel reactive and persistent.

## Scene to Case Relationship

Cases are made of scenes.
A case is the larger arc, while scenes are the individual steps that move it forward.

Example:
- Case: Missing clerk in the Old Quarter
- Scene 1: talk to the clerk’s employer
- Scene 2: inspect the lantern near the archive
- Scene 3: interview a shrine keeper
- Scene 4: follow a contradiction into the Underways
- Scene 5: reveal the hidden cause
- Scene 6: deal with the fallout

## Scene Flow

A scene should usually have a simple internal flow:

1. Entry
   The player arrives with a goal or problem.

2. Discovery
   The player gathers information, faces pressure, or tests an idea.

3. Decision
   The player chooses what to do with what they learned.

4. State Change
   Something in the world updates.

5. Exit
   The scene closes and the game moves on.

## Scene Length Rule

A scene should end when it stops producing new value.
If the player has learned what they came to learn, or the situation has stabilized, the scene should close.

This prevents scenes from dragging and keeps the pace moving.

## Scene Success Criteria

A scene is successful if it changes at least one of:
- information
- access
- reputation
- leverage
- city state
- case state

## Scene Stalling

A scene can stall if:
- the player lacks enough information
- the NPC refuses to cooperate
- the player cannot access the location
- the clue is unclear
- the city state makes progress impossible

When a scene stalls, it should usually redirect into another scene rather than dead-end.

## Design Rule

A scene is the smallest unit of meaningful change.
A case is the larger structure that organizes scenes into an arc.
The city is the persistent world that remembers the outcome of every scene and case.
