# Lantern City — Core Mechanics

## Purpose

These mechanics support a text-first investigative RPG where the city, factions, and NPCs all behave like agents with their own goals.

The mechanics should make the player feel like they are:
- gathering truth from partial evidence
- managing relationships and reputation
- influencing a living city-state
- coping with uncertainty, memory loss, and social pressure

## Design Pillars

1. Investigation over combat
2. State changes over stat inflation
3. Social consequence over raw power
4. Uncertainty as a core resource
5. The city responds to player action

## Core Player Resources

### Clues
Concrete pieces of information the player has discovered.
Clues can be true, incomplete, misleading, or contextual.

### Reputation
How different groups view the player.
Track reputation by faction and by district.
Reputation opens or closes doors.

### Leverage
Useful pressure points gained through secrets, favors, debts, evidence, or social positioning.
Leverage can be spent to force a meeting, secure access, change a story, or prevent an outcome.

### Time
Time is an important pressure mechanic.
Every major action can advance the state of a case, district, or faction plan.

### Attention
How much heat the player has drawn from powerful groups.
High attention makes the city more reactive, more dangerous, and harder to maneuver.

## City State Tracks

These are global or district-level variables the game should track persistently.

### Lantern Stability
Represents how secure and coherent a district feels.
Low stability causes:
- memory drift
- conflicting witness reports
- navigation problems
- missing records
- strange social behavior

### Civic Trust
Represents how much the city’s institutions still feel believable.
Low trust means people rely more on rumor, ritual, and private networks.

### Missingness Pressure
Represents how active the setting’s central mystery is in a given area.
Higher pressure means the world becomes less reliable.

### Faction Tension
Represents conflict between groups.
High tension creates opportunities, but also raises the risk of violence, sabotage, and political blowback.

## NPC State

Each important NPC should have a state profile with:
- trust in the player
- fear
- loyalty to a faction
- current objective
- hidden objective
- memory of promises and betrayals
- what they know
- what they suspect

NPCs should update state after each scene.
They should pursue their goals even when the player is absent.

## Scene Resolution Model

Most scenes should resolve through one of five action types:

### Observe
Gather clues, notice inconsistencies, identify patterns.

### Converse
Ask questions, negotiate, persuade, threaten, or deflect.

### Act
Move the world physically: travel, steal, hide, retrieve, repair, sabotage.

### Leverage
Use evidence, favors, reputation, or secrets to force an outcome.

### Ritual
Use lantern knowledge, shrine practice, or symbolic acts to alter state.

## Resolution Principle

The game does not need traditional dice-heavy combat resolution.
Instead, the main question is:

Can the player achieve the desired change in state with the information, leverage, and access they currently have?

If not, the game should push them toward:
- learning more
- making a deal
- changing the social context
- trying another angle

## Mystery Structure

Each mystery should be represented as a hidden chain of state changes.
Example chain:
- a person goes missing from a ledger
- witnesses begin disagreeing
- a lantern dims in the district
- a faction covers up the discrepancy
- an NPC alters their story
- the player uncovers the real cause

This means mysteries are not single puzzles.
They are living systems with moving parts.

## Information Quality

Not all information should be equally reliable.
Track whether a clue is:
- direct
- secondhand
- inferred
- ritual-derived
- corrupted
- contradicted

This lets the game create uncertainty without becoming random.

## Consequence Engine

Every major player action should do at least one of the following:
- change a relationship
- alter a district state
- expose or conceal information
- shift faction tension
- advance or stall a mystery
- increase or reduce attention

The world should feel responsive even when the player is not physically present.

## Suggested Core Loop for Mechanics

1. A case is introduced
2. The player collects partial truths
3. NPCs react based on goals and memory
4. The player spends reputation, leverage, or time
5. The district state changes
6. New information becomes available
7. The case closes, mutates, or expands

## What Makes Lantern City Different

The key mechanical difference is that light is not cosmetic.
Lanterns affect:
- memory stability
- social order
- evidence reliability
- route certainty
- institutional legitimacy

That makes illumination a systemic force, not just atmosphere.

## Open Questions

- How explicit should hidden state be to the player?
- Should clues be stored as a journal, map, or network graph?
- How much should the player see of NPC objectives?
- Should lantern rituals be rare and powerful, or common and subtle?
- How much of Missingness should be explainable versus unknowable?
