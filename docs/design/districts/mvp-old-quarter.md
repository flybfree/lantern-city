# Lantern City — MVP District Spec: Old Quarter

## Purpose

This document defines the Old Quarter as a fully specified MVP district.
It translates the high-level setting concept into a playable investigation space that supports:
- the opening case, The Missing Clerk
- district-level state
- clear social logic
- lantern-linked uncertainty
- memorable locations
- clue discovery and contradiction
- access gating
- persistent fallout

This district should function as the primary playable area for the MVP.
It is the first district the player should learn to read.

## District Summary

The Old Quarter is the city’s memory-heavy core.
It is a district of old stone, damp records, inherited obligations, narrow shrines, and institutions older than their current justifications.
People come here when they need proof, absolution, a name restored, or a story buried in respectable form.

In play, the Old Quarter should feel like:
- civic history under strain
- official continuity with visible cracks
- quiet ritual authority
- socially cautious investigation
- a place where truth is stored, edited, and doubted at the same time

## Role in the MVP

The Old Quarter should prove that Lantern City can support a district that is:
- atmospheric
- mechanically distinct
- clue-rich
- socially layered
- small enough for the MVP
- deep enough to feel alive

For the MVP, the Old Quarter should carry most of the first case.
That means it must support:
- the opening hook
- the first useful NPC conversation
- the first lantern inspection
- the first record contradiction
- one hidden route
- one hidden chamber or restricted truth space
- district-level fallout after case closure

## District Identity

### Public identity
The Old Quarter is where the city keeps old names, old agreements, old shrines, and the records that give institutions continuity.
It is respected, but not entirely trusted.
People speak of it as necessary in the way they speak of funerals, judges, and inherited debt.

### Lived identity
To residents and workers, the Old Quarter is a district where:
- every document seems heavier than it should be
- everyone knows the difference between public memory and useful memory
- old stone and weak light make uncertainty feel physical
- mistakes are rarely admitted directly
- a wrong ledger line can alter a life

### Mechanical identity
In play, the Old Quarter should specialize in:
- contradictory information
- clue comparison
- access-gated records and rooms
- NPCs who speak carefully and indirectly
- lantern effects that distort confidence rather than explode into spectacle

## Governing Powers

The district should feel governed by overlapping authorities rather than one simple ruler.

### Primary practical authority
- Memory Keepers

They control:
- archives
- record access
- registry procedures
- document legitimacy

### Primary symbolic and local stabilizing authority
- Shrine Circles

They influence:
- local lantern maintenance
- ritual interpretation
- neighborhood legitimacy at the street level
- what local people consider spiritually or socially safe

### Secondary official authority
- Council of Lights representatives or allied civic officers

They influence:
- official lantern compliance
- permitted repair narratives
- public order framing
- administrative pressure during visible disturbances

## Local Economy

The Old Quarter does not feel like a district of fast money.
Its economy should be based on continuity work.

### Dominant economic functions
- recordkeeping
- archival copying
- legal and civic certification
- ritual maintenance
- restoration and repair of old civic materials
- services tied to inheritance, property, names, and registration

### Everyday workers in the district
- clerks
- copyists
- shrine attendants
- maintenance runners
- registrars
- messengers
- conservators
- petitioners-for-hire

### Economic pressure in the MVP
The district should visibly depend on trust in records.
That means even a small records anomaly threatens:
- livelihoods
- reputations
- access rights
- old claims and family standing

This makes The Missing Clerk case feel locally important even before it becomes politically dangerous.

## Social Rules and Norms

The Old Quarter should have a distinct social grammar.

### Core social norms
- speak carefully
- do not accuse without support
- public embarrassment is treated as disorder
- rank matters, but competence matters too
- records are treated respectfully even when privately doubted
- rituals are tolerated, deferred to, or quietly feared depending on class and role

### Player-facing effect
This means the district rewards:
- precise questions
- corroborated claims
- polite persistence
- evidence-backed pressure

And punishes:
- theatrical confrontation too early
- sloppy accusations
- treating all authority as interchangeable
- assuming every answer will be direct

### District mood rule
Even when the district is tense, people should remain controlled.
Open panic is rare.
What rises instead is:
- hesitation
- contradiction
- evasion
- procedural narrowing
- strategic politeness

## Lantern Profile

The Old Quarter should be the player’s first real lesson in how lanterns affect truth.

### Baseline district lantern condition
- dim, with pockets of flickering instability around record-heavy spaces

### What this means in play
- testimony feels less certain near affected nodes
- players encounter contradiction earlier here than in a more stable district
- routes feel navigable, but not fully trustworthy
- records and memory appear close enough to stable that institutions can still deny there is a real problem

### Signature lantern feel
The Old Quarter’s light should feel:
- warm but thinning
- official but tired
- reverent but compromised
- intimate rather than grand

### Social effects of the district lantern state
- people lower their voices around disputed facts
- witnesses hedge more often
- workers prefer procedural language over direct statements
- rumors move sideways through the district faster than formal notices do

### Mechanical lantern effects for the MVP
The district should be a good place to show these effects:
- clue reliability shifts from credible to uncertain or contradicted
- NPC confidence varies by location
- some routes or rooms become accessible only with the right trust or timing
- physical evidence becomes more valuable than unsupported testimony

## Hidden Problem

### Surface version
The district is suffering a local lantern outage tied to a missing records clerk.
This is inconvenient, embarrassing, and dangerous to public confidence.

### Real district problem
The district’s systems for memory and legitimacy are vulnerable to deliberate manipulation.
The same structures meant to preserve continuity can be used to deny it.

### Why this matters
The Old Quarter’s hidden problem is not only corruption.
It is that the district’s core institutions are capable of making uncertainty look official.
That is what gives Lantern City its identity.

## Active MVP Problems

For the MVP, the Old Quarter should begin with these visible or semi-visible district problems:
- disputed archive record access
- unstable lantern near archive-related streets
- rising rumor pressure around a missing clerk
- quiet blame shifting between shrine and archive authority
- witness inconsistency inside a small affected radius

These problems should make the district feel active even before the player resolves the case.

## District Entry Experience

The player’s first impression of the Old Quarter should tell them exactly what kind of district this is.

### What they should notice on entry
- old stone under constant damp
- warm lantern glass that never fully brightens the street
- notices layered over older notices
- official spaces that feel narrow rather than grand
- pedestrians who pause before answering specific questions
- an atmosphere of controlled uncertainty

### Entry text design rule
The district entry should imply that this place is meant to preserve continuity, while showing signs that continuity is failing.

## Playable MVP Locations

The Old Quarter should include a small but rich location network.
Each location should have a distinct play function.

## 1. Archive Steps

### Role
- opening location
- public access point
- first institutional friction point

### Function in play
This is where the player first meets record authority, contradiction, and procedural obstruction.
It should introduce the district’s social logic immediately.

### Sensory identity
- wet stone steps
- dim official lantern
- posted notices with subtle inconsistencies
- controlled foot traffic
- doors that imply access hierarchy

### Likely interactions
- speak to Sered Marr
- inspect notice board
- compare public postings
- ask workers about Tovin Vale
- request record access

### Clue utility
Good place for:
- contradictory roster entry
- visible sign of notice replacement
- witness hesitation

## 2. Shrine Lane

### Role
- first lantern-investigation space
- first meaningful informal authority space
- emotional and atmospheric counterweight to archive formality

### Function in play
This is where the player first gets a useful lantern-informed interpretation of events.
It should feel quieter, more local, and more honest than the archive, but not fully transparent.

### Sensory identity
- close stone walls
- dripping awnings
- the smell of oil and wet soot
- small shrine architecture
- a lantern that appears stable until examined more closely

### Likely interactions
- speak to Ila Venn
- inspect lantern bracket
- ask about outage timing
- ask about recent repairs
- request access to a workroom

### Clue utility
Good place for:
- outage predates disappearance
- heat scoring on bracket
- false repair pattern

## 3. Ledger Room / Registry Desk

### Role
- records contradiction site
- access-gated interior
- clue comparison space

### Function in play
This location should convert suspicion into documentary evidence.
It is less visually dramatic than Shrine Lane, but mechanically crucial.

### Sensory identity
- damp paper smell
- ink, scraping chairs, ordered shelves
- low conversation, tense procedure
- narrow pools of light over desks

### Likely interactions
- compare ledger lines
- request restricted viewing
- challenge Sered with contradiction
- search for maintenance omission
- identify hand mismatch or document irregularity

### Clue utility
Good place for:
- missing maintenance line
- family-entry discrepancy
- evidence of suppression

## 4. Service Passage

### Role
- hidden route
- movement breakthrough
- physical proof location

### Function in play
This is where the district becomes spatially investigative rather than only social and documentary.
It should reward persistence and tied-together clues.

### Sensory identity
- tight stone passage
- metal latch wear
- old maintenance rails or hooks
- trapped damp air
- light that behaves differently from the street above

### Unlock conditions
This route should open through some combination of:
- trust from Ila Venn
- evidence from Brin Hesse
- leverage over records or maintenance access
- careful inspection of route clues

### Clue utility
Good place for:
- moved service latch
- unauthorized tool marks
- hidden copy sheet

## 5. Subarchive Chamber

### Role
- breakthrough location
- victim recovery space
- truth chamber

### Function in play
This location should pay off the district’s themes in a human-scale way.
The player should feel that all the district’s contradictions converge here.

### Sensory identity
- cramped, stale space
- improvised safety
- copied pages and survival traces
- uneasy quiet rather than dramatic menace

### Clue utility
Good place for:
- Tovin Vale
- copied registry fragments
- direct testimony

## 6. Courtyard Threshold or Side Chapel Exterior

### Role
- optional transition / rumor / pressure space
- pause point between archive and shrine spheres

### Function in play
This location can be used for:
- overheard rumor
- authority encounter
- district mood updates
- noncritical but flavorful redirection

## District Movement Model

The Old Quarter should be small enough to understand quickly.
The player should not feel lost geographically.

### Suggested movement structure
- Archive Steps as public anchor
- Shrine Lane as first side lead
- Ledger Room as restricted but central interior
- Service Passage as hidden route unlocked later
- Subarchive Chamber as late-case location
- one threshold or transitional exterior space for pacing and rumor

### Movement rule
Travel inside the district should usually be fast, but not meaningless.
If nothing new happens, movement can resolve compactly.
If pressure, rumor, or access matters, movement should become a short transition scene.

## Access Patterns

The Old Quarter should teach the player that access is progression.

### Public access
Available early:
- Archive Steps exterior
- Shrine Lane exterior
- general district observation

### Restricted access
Requires trust, leverage, or procedural permission:
- Ledger Room interior
- shrine workroom
- maintenance records

### Hidden access
Requires clue convergence or route knowledge:
- Service Passage
- Subarchive Chamber

### Design goal
The district should never feel blocked at random.
If access is denied, the player should understand why and what kind of progress might change that.

## NPC Distribution in the District

The Old Quarter should feel inhabited, but the MVP should focus on a tight cluster of meaningful people.

### Primary case-linked NPCs in district
- Sered Marr at Archive Steps / Ledger Room
- Ila Venn at Shrine Lane
- Brin Hesse moving between service routes and maintenance edges
- Tovin Vale in hidden chamber late in case

### Secondary pressure or authority visitors
- Watcher Pell can enter at archive-adjacent spaces when pressure rises
- Sister Calis can appear through shrine-linked or authority-linked access later

### Ambient/local NPC types
- petitioners waiting on records
- copyists leaving for the day
- shrine visitors
- maintenance hands who know when not to speak
- residents with half-stable memories of recent disturbance

## Rumor Pool

The Old Quarter should have a rumor pool that reinforces uncertainty without giving away the whole case.

### Good rumor lines for the MVP
- “There was a clerk there, unless he was borrowed from another hall.”
- “The notice changed after the rain started, not before.”
- “The shrine lamp was wrong before anyone admitted it.”
- “People keep blaming neglect because neglect is easier to file.”
- “Records go bad here quietly. That is what makes them dangerous.”
- “If the wrong line disappears, so do the people attached to it.”

### Rumor design rule
Rumors should be evocative, slanted, and partial.
They should help the player think, not replace investigation.

## Typical Case Patterns in This District

The Old Quarter should naturally support cases involving:
- missing people tied to records
- inheritance disputes with unreliable documentation
- shrine-linked lantern anomalies
- erased or altered civic identities
- hidden maintenance manipulation
- institutions preserving order by narrowing truth

This makes it a strong anchor district for the whole game, not just the MVP.

## Distinctive Gameplay Pressures

The Old Quarter should feel different from other districts because it pressures the player in specific ways.

### 1. Information contradiction pressure
The player will often have multiple plausible but unstable versions of an event.
Progress comes from comparison, not simple discovery.

### 2. Social caution pressure
NPCs often know useful things, but will not state them bluntly.
The player must read implication and ask precise follow-ups.

### 3. Access pressure
The most useful truths are behind doors, desks, procedures, or social trust.

### 4. Legitimacy pressure
The district cares deeply about what can be certified.
The player is not only looking for truth.
They are learning how truth becomes official or fails to.

## District Feedback and State Labels

The district should expose a few readable state labels to the player.

### Recommended visible district labels
- Lantern condition: dim / flickering / stabilized
- District tension: stable / uneasy / strained
- Records confidence: normal / disputed / compromised
- Public mood: guarded / whispering / alarmed

These should update during or after case progress.

## Case-Specific District State Changes

The Missing Clerk should be able to change the Old Quarter in visible ways.

### Before breakthrough
- Lantern condition: dim
- District tension: uneasy
- Records confidence: disputed
- Public mood: whispering

### After strong evidence but before resolution
- Lantern condition: flickering or unstable
- District tension: strained
- Records confidence: compromised
- Public mood: guarded and alert

### After clean exposure
- Lantern condition: stabilized but not fully trusted
- District tension: high, then settling
- Records confidence: damaged but recoverable
- Public mood: openly discussing what happened

### After quiet rescue
- Lantern condition: improved locally
- District tension: moderate
- Records confidence: outwardly restored, inwardly doubted
- Public mood: uncertain but subdued

### After failure or burial
- Lantern condition: superficially stable
- District tension: low on the surface, worse underneath
- Records confidence: officially restored, privately false
- Public mood: resigned and less trusting

## How the District Teaches the Game

The Old Quarter should teach the player these core Lantern City lessons:
- not every lie is spoken aloud
- records can be clues, weapons, or masks
- lantern conditions change what evidence means
- trust opens places that force cannot easily reach in the MVP
- solving a case is partly about deciding what version of truth can survive institutionally

## Interaction Model Hooks

This district should work especially well with the hybrid interaction model.

### It should support
- structured dialogue topics around records, maintenance, and timing
- clue comparison scenes
- careful free-text questioning within bounded conversations
- strong case-board use because contradictions matter
- clear “what matters here?” recovery prompts

### Good recovery outputs in this district
- “The archive and shrine agree on less than they should.”
- “If the records are unstable, physical maintenance evidence may be more reliable.”
- “Someone with either ritual knowledge or maintenance access touched the problem before it became public.”
- “You still need either deeper access or a witness who can tie the route to the alteration.”

## Authoring Notes for Future Expansion

If the district expands beyond the MVP, the Old Quarter can support:
- family archive cases
- false inheritance chains
- shrine politics
- buried legal histories
- forgotten civic districts attached to older maps
- recurring NPCs who manage continuity for a price

But none of that is required for the first playable slice.

## MVP Build Checklist for This District

A playable MVP version of the Old Quarter should include:
- one district entry summary
- at least 4 major interactable locations
- 1 hidden route
- 1 hidden chamber
- 3 to 4 meaningful local NPC anchors
- one rumor pool
- one district state object
- one lantern state object with local anomalies
- one records-confidence state label
- one visible district fallout update after case resolution

## Design Rules

1. The Old Quarter should feel old, careful, and quietly compromised.
2. Contradiction should be a mechanic, not just atmosphere.
3. Access denial should create direction, not confusion.
4. Lantern effects should create uncertainty with structure, not chaos.
5. The district should reward precision more than aggression.
6. Public legitimacy should matter as much as hidden truth.
7. Every major location should contribute a different kind of evidence or pressure.
8. The district should feel small enough to understand, but deep enough to mistrust.

## Summary

The Old Quarter is the MVP’s primary investigation district.
It is where the player learns how Lantern City works: through unstable records, dim light, cautious conversation, and truths that must be made legible before they can be made real.

If built well, this district alone should make the game’s identity clear.
